Professional Biography

Ratnesh Kumar Yadav is an advocate enrolled with the Bar Council of India, practising before the High Court and subordinate courts. His areas of engagement encompass criminal law, constitutional matters, and civil litigation, with a practice grounded in careful reading of statute and case law.

He has appeared in matters involving personal liberty, bail proceedings, criminal trials, writ petitions, and civil disputes. His approach to practice is methodical, research-oriented, and guided by the procedural and substantive requirements of the law.

Committed to the traditions of the legal profession, Adv. Yadav maintains that the role of an advocate is to provide sound, lawful counsel and diligent representation in accordance with the Advocates Act, 1961, and the professional standards prescribed by the Bar Council of India.

Criminal Law · Educational Article

Understanding Bail under the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Bail is a fundamental concept in the administration of criminal justice, premised on the presumption of innocence until proven guilty. The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC) classifies offences into bailable and non-bailable categories, each carrying a different legal framework for the grant of bail.

Bailable Offences

Under Section 436 CrPC, a person accused of a bailable offence is entitled to bail as a matter of right. The police officer or court is bound to release such a person on bail, subject to the furnishing of sureties or a personal bond, as directed.

Non-Bailable Offences

For non-bailable offences, Section 437 CrPC grants discretion to the Magistrate to release an accused on bail, taking into account factors such as the nature of the offence, the evidence available, the antecedents of the accused, and the likelihood of tampering with witnesses or fleeing from justice.

Anticipatory Bail

Section 438 CrPC provides for anticipatory bail, enabling a person apprehending arrest to seek protection from custody in advance. The Supreme Court, in Gurbaksh Singh Sibbia v. State of Punjab (1980), interpreted this provision liberally, holding that it was not to be applied in a narrow or restrictive manner.

Bail under Article 21

The constitutional dimension of bail is rooted in Article 21, which guarantees the right to life and personal liberty. Courts have consistently held that the power to grant bail must be exercised in keeping with this constitutional mandate, and that pre-trial detention is not to be used as a form of pre-emptive punishment.

Constitutional Law · Educational Article

The Writ Jurisdiction of High Courts under Article 226

Article 226 of the Constitution of India vests High Courts with a broad power to issue writs for the enforcement of fundamental rights as well as for any other purpose. This jurisdiction is wider than that of the Supreme Court under Article 32, which is confined to the enforcement of Part III rights.

Types of Writs

The Constitution envisages five principal writs: Habeas Corpus (to secure release from unlawful detention), Mandamus (to compel performance of a public duty), Prohibition (to prevent inferior courts from exceeding jurisdiction), Certiorari (to quash orders passed without jurisdiction or in violation of natural justice), and Quo Warranto (to inquire into the legality of a person's assumption of public office).

Scope and Limitations

High Courts may issue writs against the State and its instrumentalities, as well as against private bodies performing public functions. The jurisdiction is discretionary; a petitioner must approach the court with clean hands and cannot ordinarily be heard if alternative remedies have not been exhausted, unless the matter involves a flagrant violation of fundamental rights or raises pure questions of law.

Territorial Jurisdiction

The 42nd Constitutional Amendment and subsequent decisions have clarified that a High Court may exercise writ jurisdiction over persons or authorities located within its territorial limits, or where the cause of action, even partly, arises within such territory.

Civil Law · Educational Article

Principles of Temporary Injunctions in Civil Courts

A temporary injunction is a court order directing a party to refrain from doing, or to continue doing, a particular act, pending the final disposal of a suit. The power to grant such injunctions is codified under Order XXXIX of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, read with Section 94.

The Three-Pronged Test

Indian courts consistently apply a three-part test in assessing applications for temporary injunctions, originally formulated by the Supreme Court in Dalpat Kumar v. Prahlad Singh (1992) and subsequent decisions.

Prima Facie Case: The applicant must demonstrate that there exists a bona fide dispute and an arguable question of law or fact, raising a prima facie case in their favour. The court does not, at this stage, conduct a final assessment of merits.

Balance of Convenience: The court must weigh the relative inconvenience to each party. Where refusal of injunction would cause greater hardship to the applicant than its grant would cause to the respondent, the balance tilts in favour of granting it.

Irreparable Injury: If the injunction is not granted, the applicant must stand to suffer harm that cannot be adequately compensated by damages. Mere monetary loss, readily quantifiable, may ordinarily be compensated and may not justify injunctive relief.

Suppression of Material Facts

Courts have held that a party seeking equitable relief by way of injunction must approach with full disclosure. Suppression of material facts may disentitle a party from the discretionary relief sought, regardless of the merits of the substantive claim.
