Criminal Law · Educational Article

Understanding Bail under the CrPC, 1973

Bail is a fundamental concept in criminal justice, premised on the presumption of innocence until proven guilty. The CrPC classifies offences into bailable and non-bailable categories.

Bailable Offences

Under Section 436, a person accused of a bailable offence is entitled to bail as a matter of right. The officer or court is bound to release such a person upon furnishing sureties or a personal bond.

Non-Bailable Offences

For non-bailable offences, Section 437 grants the Magistrate discretion to release an accused on bail, considering the nature of the offence, evidence, antecedents, and likelihood of flight or witness tampering.

Anticipatory Bail

Section 438 provides for anticipatory bail for persons apprehending arrest. The Supreme Court in Gurbaksh Singh Sibbia v. State of Punjab (1980) held it was not to be applied narrowly or restrictively.

Bail and Article 21

Courts have consistently held that bail must be considered in keeping with Article 21's guarantee of life and personal liberty, and that pre-trial detention is not to serve as pre-emptive punishment.

Constitutional Law · Educational Article

Writ Jurisdiction of High Courts under Article 226

Article 226 vests High Courts with broad power to issue writs for the enforcement of fundamental rights as well as for any other purpose — wider than the Supreme Court's jurisdiction under Article 32.

Types of Writs

The five principal writs are: Habeas Corpus (release from unlawful detention), Mandamus (compelling public duty), Prohibition (preventing inferior courts from exceeding jurisdiction), Certiorari (quashing orders without jurisdiction), and Quo Warranto (inquiring into assumption of public office).

Scope and Discretion

The jurisdiction is discretionary. A petitioner must approach with clean hands and ordinarily cannot be heard if alternative remedies have not been exhausted, unless the matter involves a flagrant violation of fundamental rights.

Territorial Jurisdiction

A High Court may exercise writ jurisdiction over persons within its territorial limits, or where the cause of action, even partly, arises within such territory.

Civil Law · Educational Article

Principles of Temporary Injunctions in Civil Courts

A temporary injunction directs a party to refrain from, or continue, a particular act pending the final disposal of a suit. The power is codified under Order XXXIX of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908.

The Three-Pronged Test

Prima Facie Case: The applicant must demonstrate an arguable question of law or fact. The court does not conduct a final assessment of merits at this stage.

Balance of Convenience: The court weighs relative inconvenience. Where refusal causes greater hardship to the applicant than its grant causes to the respondent, the balance favours granting it.

Irreparable Injury: The applicant must face harm not adequately compensated by damages alone. Readily quantifiable monetary loss may not justify injunctive relief.

Suppression of Material Facts

Suppression of material facts may disentitle a party from discretionary relief, regardless of the merits of the substantive claim.
